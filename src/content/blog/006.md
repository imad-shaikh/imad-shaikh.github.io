Transformers are one of the most important ideas behind modern AI systems, especially large language models. Models like ChatGPT are built on transformer architecture, which is designed to process sequences of information and use context to make predictions.

A GPT model stands for Generative Pre-Trained Transformer. "Generative" means it creates new text, "pre-trained" means it was trained on large amounts of data before being used, and "transformer" refers to the neural network architecture that makes the model work.

At a high level, a GPT-style model takes in text and predicts what should come next. That might sound simple, but repeated next-token prediction is what allows the model to generate full paragraphs, answer questions, write code, and carry on conversations.

## From Text to Tokens

Before a transformer can process text, the input has to be broken into smaller pieces called tokens. A token can be a word, part of a word, punctuation, or another small chunk of text.

For example, a sentence is not passed into the model as raw English. It is first converted into a sequence of tokens, and each token is then turned into a vector: a list of numbers that the model can process.

This matters because neural networks do not understand words directly. They operate on numbers. The process of turning tokens into vectors is called embedding.

## What Are Embeddings?

An embedding is a numerical representation of a token. One way to think about embeddings is that they place tokens into a high-dimensional space, where tokens with related meanings may end up closer together.

For example, words related to cities, emotions, programming, or food may develop similar patterns in their vector representations. These patterns are not hand-written by engineers. They are learned during training.

The matrix responsible for turning tokens into vectors is called the embedding matrix. It starts with random values, but as the model trains on data, those values are adjusted so the embeddings become more useful.

This is one of the key ideas in deep learning: instead of explicitly programming every rule, we give the model flexible parameters, called weights, and train those weights using data.

## The Transformer's Main Structure

Once tokens become vectors, they move through the transformer. The transformer is made of repeating blocks, mainly involving two important parts: attention layers and feed-forward layers.

The attention layer allows tokens to exchange information with each other. This is what helps the model understand context. A word can mean different things depending on the words around it, and attention helps the model decide which surrounding tokens matter most.

The feed-forward layer, sometimes called a multilayer perceptron, processes each vector individually. Unlike attention, this step does not make tokens communicate with each other. Instead, it applies learned transformations to each vector to refine the information it contains.

These layers repeat many times. As the vectors move deeper through the network, they become less like simple word representations and more like context-rich representations of the input.

## Why Context Matters

The same word can mean different things in different sentences. A transformer needs to update each token's representation based on the surrounding context.

At the beginning, a token vector only represents the token itself. After moving through attention and feed-forward layers, that vector can carry much more information: its meaning, its role in the sentence, and how it relates to the larger input.

This is one reason transformers became so powerful. They are not just looking at words one at a time. They are constantly adjusting representations based on context.

## Predicting the Next Token

After the input has moved through the transformer layers, the model needs to produce an output. In a GPT-style model, the goal is to predict the next token.

To do this, the final vector is passed through another learned matrix called the unembedding matrix. This maps the model's internal representation back into a list of scores, one for each possible token in the vocabulary.

These raw scores are called logits. By themselves, logits are not probabilities. Some may be negative, some may be large, and they do not necessarily add up to one.

That is where softmax comes in.

## Softmax and Probabilities

Softmax converts a list of raw scores into a probability distribution. After softmax, every value is between 0 and 1, and all the values add up to 1.

This lets the model assign probabilities to possible next tokens. The model might decide that one token is very likely, a few others are somewhat likely, and many others are unlikely.

When generating text, the model can sample from this distribution. Then the chosen token is added to the input, and the process repeats. This is how a language model can generate text one token at a time.

## The Big Picture

A transformer takes text, breaks it into tokens, turns those tokens into vectors, updates those vectors using attention and feed-forward layers, and finally predicts what token should come next.

The important idea is that the model is not following hand-written grammar rules or memorizing simple responses. It is using learned weights, matrix operations, embeddings, attention, and probability distributions to model patterns in language.

This is why transformers are central to modern AI. They provide a flexible architecture for handling context, learning from large datasets, and generating useful outputs across many different tasks.
