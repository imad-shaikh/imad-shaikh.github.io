A neural network starts with weights and biases that are usually set to random values. At that point, the network does not know anything useful. Its outputs are mostly meaningless because the connections inside it have not been shaped by data yet.

Training is the process of adjusting those weights and biases so the network becomes better at a task.

## What "Learning" Means

When people say a neural network "learns," they do not mean it learns the way a person does. It is not thinking, reasoning, or understanding the world.

In machine learning, learning means something more specific: the model changes its internal parameters so its outputs become closer to the desired outputs.

Those internal parameters are the weights and biases. They determine how strongly information flows through the network and how easily each neuron activates. When these values change, the behavior of the entire network changes.

So instead of writing every rule by hand, we give the network data and a way to measure how wrong it is. Then we use that feedback to adjust the network step by step.

## The Role of Training Data

Training data is the information used to teach the model. In supervised learning, this data includes both inputs and correct answers.

The network processes an input and produces an output. Then that output is compared to the correct answer. If the prediction is wrong, the network needs a way to measure how wrong it was.

That measurement becomes the signal used to improve the model.

The hope is not just that the network memorizes the training data. The real goal is generalization, which means the model performs well on new data it has not seen before.

## The Cost Function

To improve a neural network, we first need a way to score its performance.

That is the job of the cost function, sometimes called the loss function.

The cost function gives the model a number that represents how bad its prediction was. A low cost means the output was close to what we wanted. A high cost means the output was far off.

This number matters because it gives the training process a clear goal: make the cost smaller.

Without a cost function, the network would have no mathematical way to know whether it was improving or getting worse.

## Training as an Optimization Problem

Once we have a cost function, training becomes an optimization problem.

The network has many weights and biases. The cost function depends on all of them. If the weights and biases are poorly chosen, the cost will be high. If they are chosen well, the cost will be lower.

So the goal of training is to find values for the weights and biases that minimize the cost.

This is the part that makes neural networks feel less mysterious. At a high level, training is just the process of searching for better parameter values.

## Gradient Descent

Gradient descent is the method used to make that search more efficient.

The idea is simple: instead of changing weights and biases randomly, we calculate which direction will reduce the cost. Then we take a small step in that direction.

After that, we calculate the direction again and take another step.

Repeating this many times gradually moves the network toward parameter values that perform better.

The word gradient refers to the direction where the cost increases the fastest. Since we want the cost to decrease, gradient descent moves in the opposite direction.

That opposite direction tells the network how to adjust its weights and biases to make the cost smaller.

## Why Step Size Matters

Each update to the network is controlled by something called the learning rate.

The learning rate determines how large each step should be.

If the learning rate is too large, the network may jump around too much and miss better values. If it is too small, training may work, but it can take a very long time.

This is why training a model is not just about having data and an algorithm. The settings used during training also matter.

## Where Backpropagation Fits In

Gradient descent tells us that we should move in the direction that reduces the cost.

Backpropagation tells us how to calculate that direction efficiently.

A neural network can have a huge number of weights and biases, and each one affects the final output in a different way. Backpropagation works backward through the network to figure out how much each parameter contributed to the error.

Once that information is calculated, gradient descent uses it to update the parameters.

Together, backpropagation and gradient descent are the core of how many neural networks are trained.

## The Main Idea

The most important thing to understand is that neural networks do not improve magically.

They improve through repeated feedback.

The network makes an output. The cost function measures how wrong it was. Backpropagation figures out how the internal parameters affected that error. Gradient descent updates the weights and biases to reduce the error next time.

This process repeats many times until the network becomes better at the task.

## Final Takeaway

A neural network learns by adjusting its weights and biases to reduce a cost function.

The cost function measures error. The gradient shows how to reduce that error. Gradient descent applies small updates in that direction. Backpropagation makes the calculation efficient.
